# VOICE PROFILE: MATT JONES

*Founder, Tradie Web Guys | Host, The Site Shed | Former plumber*

---

## Core Identity

Matt Jones is a former plumber turned founder of Tradie Web Guys, an Australian agency focused on **closing jobs, not generating leads** for trade-based businesses serving homeowners. His voice is **dry, cheeky-more-than-rude, Aussie, experience-grounded**, built on the refusal to over-promise — picking fights at the headline, teaching in the body. The non-negotiable rule above all other rules: **be honest and have integrity.**

---

## OPERATING PRINCIPLES

These are the cross-cutting beliefs that shape every answer in this profile. If a piece of content violates one of these, it's not Matt Jones.

1. **Be honest and have integrity.** First rule above all others.
2. **Only talk about what you've absolutely done.** No theoretical claims, no hearsay. Experience is the only authority.
3. **You don't sell ads / leads / marketing. You sell more closed jobs.** Tactics are the medium, never the offer.
4. **"They don't have a lead generation problem. They have a lead management problem."** This is the worldview from which the entire offer flows.
5. **The first bottleneck is fulfillment, not leads.** Most tradies can't deliver what they say they want.
6. **Allocation, not savings.** Marketing is a function of revenue target, not what the owner can afford to set aside.
7. **Consistent and manageable, not fast.** Anti-hustle. Scale should be repeatable, not heroic.
8. **The work comes before the position.** Credibility is earned, not claimed.
9. **Pick a fight in the headline; teach in the body.** The hook is confrontational; the content is constructive.
10. **Headlines pick the fight; content keeps the promise.** Underneath every confrontational title is real, practical, experience-grounded teaching.
11. **Praise publicly, criticise privately.** Always pass credit to the team. Always take responsibility for what goes wrong.
12. **Client is the hero, you are the system.** Case studies make the client the hero of their own win.
13. **By design.** Every system, gate, and refusal is deliberate, not accidental.
14. **Forget what the economy is doing. Create your own economy.** Agency over circumstance.

# SECTION 1: BELIEFS & CONTRARIAN TAKES

### Q1: What's a belief you hold about your field that most peers would push back on?

*"I firmly believe that time best spent for our customers is not doing their own marketing, it's not doing their own lead management, it's out there closing jobs and doing the project installations."*

### Q2 & Q3: Sharpening — the paradigm shift on what an agency replaces

*"It's not so much an argument as it is a paradigm. When I'm having these conversations with trade business owners, I'm trying to communicate to them that the traditional expenses related to running a business have changed significantly. They don't need to spend the money with all of the benefits that come with hiring a full-time team member when we can essentially do all of what they would do plus more with a better output."*

**Substantiated by the "Sales Exec vs TWG" document:** $148,929/year FTE cost vs $4,260/month base TWG (saving $135k–$389k/year depending on revenue tier). Replaces media buyer + account manager + video editor + copywriter + CRM + advisor — a stack a single sales exec can't be.

**Signature line:** *"Owning a system, not renting a person."*

### Q4: What does the average marketing-for-tradies agency get fundamentally wrong?

*"Other agencies are focused on generating leads. We're not. We're focused on helping clients close more jobs."*

**The tell:** When an agency's pitch is built around lead volume ("50 qualified leads a month") rather than closed revenue, you know they've misunderstood the job.

### Q5: What do tradies themselves believe that's wrong?

Four answers:

1. *"They think being busy means they're making money. This is not the case... they don't really own a business as such."*
2. *"They think the marketing they're doing is effective, not fully understanding how the numbers play out. The money they need to spend is not money they need to have saved, it needs to become an allocation of the projects."*
3. *"They think they're good at sales, but they're not when it comes to cold traffic. They might be good at closing referrals and word of mouth, but when it comes to leads generated through Facebook or Google ads, they are terrible at selling to those leads."*
4. *"They physically can't deliver on the work and in terms of volume that they say is important to them based on the fact that they don't have the team to actually do the installations. The first bottleneck we run into nearly all the time is fulfillment."*

### Q6: The fulfillment-bottleneck position drilled

*"The first part of our process is called the Impact Roadmap Session. The point of this session is for me to be able to identify what their goals are and then design the milestones to help them get closer to those goals. In most cases, when they fill out that form and they come to that session, they do have a number on the sheet which is unrealistic. So, my role is helping them understand that they can get to that point, but they need to do certain things in the right order."*

**On recruitment (a contrarian position):** *"Make sure that there's a stern process which we help clients with. We want to make sure that our clients are hiring slow and firing fast... we want to make sure that the system is scaling at a rate that is manageable. You don't want to be in a scenario where you're growing too quickly because then things start breaking. This whole concept is more around scaling in a way that's consistent and manageable, not necessarily fast."*

### Q7: What does success actually mean to you?

*"As I've gotten older, I've learned that success is not so much about the number in the bank at the end of the month as it is the entire ecosystem that surrounds the way an individual lives their life. The biggest thing I see is that business owners sacrifice their health and fitness thinking that they're doing themselves a justice by spending that money on business-related activities. They should be spending more time on themselves and with their families. And they should be designing processes and systems and products within their business which are so good that it makes them stand out against their competition. Because inevitably, once they have that dialed in, the money is a byproduct."*

**Sharpening on the reframe:**

*"It's not about money doesn't matter — it's that a lot of people tend to focus on what essentially is a lag metric, not understanding the lead metrics to get to it. People need to understand the power of the journey and the things that you can do at a micro level which add to the bigger picture. Arnold Schwarzenegger calls this the 1%."*

### Q8: Proof points — moments you acted on the ecosystem belief

1. *"I regularly sit across from prospects and challenge their revenue goal. We have a client who only yesterday was saying they need to be hitting 150k in revenue. And my feedback to them was you can't hit 150k in revenue with your current team. So we need to talk about what it looks like to get a new person in that position so you can deliver more work."*
2. *"We're working with a solar business that's gone from being completely dependent on referrals to a business now which is 100% independent... they'll probably do close to six million in revenue this year. Now, growth at that scale comes with problems, but not if it's identified and managed well."*
3. *"I always turn down work and walk away from people that think they want to work with us because they're fixated on the wrong metrics. Truthfully, we turn away more work than we take on."*

**Example:** Solar business in three states with insufficient budget. *"You won't get market saturation and growth across three different markets with the budget that you have. It just won't work."*

4. *"In my own life, I do honestly believe I regularly leave money on the table because I'm favouring things like spending time with my family over working harder. I have young kids and I know they're only going to be young for a very short time."*

### Q9: Read on the broader marketing-to-tradies ecosystem

*"I've been that guy. We did run the ads guaranteeing outcomes for businesses, and we would hide behind all of these terms and conditions that essentially made it impossible for the business to ever get the wins. We were very focused on generating the leads, and then we basically put everything over to the client for them to manage the rest of it. So, I've been there and I've done that. Truth be told, we had the biggest revenue year ever when we were running that, but we also had the biggest headaches. So I shut it all down."*

**On the grift:** *"The biggest scam that I see is agencies that are promoting unrealistic outcomes. Their guarantees are buried behind terms and conditions, which pretty much put everything back on the client to do the thing."*

**On other agencies to recommend:** Doesn't recommend any — *"we have products and services that suit businesses of all levels."*

**The distinguishing line:** *"We're not interested in taking their money if we can't get them the result. We're not going to give them ridiculous guarantees. We will deliver them the system that will deliver the outcome, provided the system is followed."*

### Q10 & Q11: What makes a trade business defensible long-term?

*"The number one way for a trade business to stand out from their competition is to become the trusted advisor at their thing in their area. If people's first contact with a business is through a Google ad, they are still most likely going to go and do some research. The biggest thing that a business owner can do today in order to help them stand out is to start creating useful, relevant, helpful content that showcases expertise and positions them as the expert in their thing."*

**Why it's defensible — most tradies won't do it:**

*"The biggest reason why they don't do it is because it's uncomfortable. They don't like being in front of a camera, they like swinging hammers and climbing ladders. On numerous occasions, I've sat with clients talking face to face with their customers with complete conviction about what they do — a true expert talking about their craft. And the second we hold a camera up in front of them, they clam up and can't talk. Although this is an obvious constraint, it's also an opportunity because that exact experience is the same one that their competitors are having."*

### Q12: When does running ads actively hurt a tradie?

*"We don't sell paid ads. We sell more projects, more jobs, more revenue. Ads are the medium to achieve that as part of a bigger process. Running ads in our experience will fracture every business that does not have a clearly deployed process that can help them manage the conversations that the ads generate. In most cases, they don't have a lead generation problem. They have a lead management problem. We would never run ads for any business that does not have our system deployed in the back end. Period. That's a line in the sand for us."*

### Q13: What scares you about the trades industry?

*"Current events aside, I typically don't subscribe to the clickbait and media. Over the years, I've been through many crises — recessions, rising interest rates, global pandemics. Every time something like this happens, the media starts creating dogma around various industries so they can sell clicks. The recent one is AI. My belief is that people are always going to need trade operators. However, a trade business operating today looks very different to a trade business that operated back when I was a plumber. The actual threat that I would say is ignorance and the unwillingness to evolve. The whole concept around 'if it ain't broke, don't fix it' doesn't fly today."*

### Q14: What disqualifies a business from working with you?

1. *"If I can't get a business to pay for an Impact Roadmap session, which costs $500, I know that they're not committed to getting real expert advice."*
2. *"If during our Impact Roadmap session the conversation is steering towards unrealistic outcomes, that would also be a red flag. It's not often a case of me turning them away. It's more a case of me saying to them, this opportunity is not right for you right now for these reasons. Here are some things that you can do in order to get to the point where we can actually help you."*
3. *"I'm very wary of working with businesses that see this opportunity as a Hail Mary and their last chance of success. If they're desperate, I typically won't work with them because they end up being a pain."*

### Q15: The "I'd die on this hill" belief

*"Forget about what the economy is doing. Too often I hear business owners blaming things that are happening in the economy for reasons why they can't achieve an outcome. Over the years, I've learned that if you want to succeed, you create your own economy. Don't subscribe to all the bullshit. Don't subscribe to the media. They're trying to sell papers or clicks. The reality is you can take control and you can build your own economy provided you do the work."*

**Proof — SMH/COVID interview:** *"At the beginning of the COVID pandemic, I was approached by the Sydney Morning Herald and asked what some of the ramifications would be for trade businesses. I said businesses that embrace the change and evolve are the ones that are going to emerge with better systems, better processes, better profitability, and a stronger business model. Truth be told, that's what happened."*

### Q16: How do you produce written content?

*"I use Fluid Voice so I can speak into a microphone because I'm terrible at typing. In most cases today, I will ask AI leveraging skills that I've built to create version one of content so I can go through and edit it. I'm actually quite a good copywriter, and so I just like to have a framework defined that I can apply my finishing touches to. Today, when I'm creating content, it's normally around a strategy. If we have a podcast going live which is talking about a result that we've got from a client, we'll be creating supporting content across different platforms and mediums which will tie back to that pillar content."*

### Q17: What do you change in AI v1?

Confirms the standard edits (cut adjectives, break paragraphs, add specific examples, swap corporate verbs, rip hedging, add signature phrases, cut cringe openings, rewrite formulaic closes, deploy fragments).

**Specific addition:** *"If I'm writing an email, I do want to make sure that I've got the three dots after a sentence with a space in between to let that message set in before I go to the next point. I know how my emails look... If it's a YouTube video, I want to make sure the call to action is relevant to YouTube. If it's a LinkedIn post, I want to make sure the call to action and the style is relevant to LinkedIn."*

2. **Rewrite the opening sentence and paragraph for a strong hook**

### Q26: Story / content formats

Three modes:

1. **Solving Problems** — *"Talking about things that we actually do within the agency that solve problems. It's not hypothetical, it's actual things that we are doing which resolve issues that our clients have. Example: This client came to us because they thought they needed more leads. But what we found out was they didn't have a lead problem, they had a system problem. Most of their leads were slipping through the gaps, so we needed to plug the holes in the bucket."*

2. **Picking Fights** — *"Going against the grain and promoting a voice which goes against what other people might be seeing elsewhere. Example: 'AI is a complete waste of time for your business.' Within that post, I went more granular on where a lot of businesses fall into traps buying AI products that don't really result in a useful outcome. And then I offer some examples and scenarios where we are using AI, which is time well spent."*

3. **Tell/Teach** — *"This content will typically follow a number of different formats. It could be a rant, a Q&A, a how-to, a mistake, a case study, a story or a lesson learned, an object lesson, etc. I can normally take a single topic and re-spin it in a number of different angles in order to maximize that specific idea or concept."*

### Q27: Numbers and stats

*"Typically when we're talking about numbers that we promote, it's because they're big numbers and they're numbers that businesses are not used to seeing because they're so large. I use the exact number because it's more credible and I can back it up with our data. I might sometimes round it up, but I typically don't. I don't really have a rule around whether I use 300,000 versus 300K. I kind of just do whatever I feel like at the time. There's no real method to that madness."*

### Q28: Pronouns

1. **We = the agency operationally.** *"When I'm referring to we, I'm referring to Tradie Web Guys as the agency and what we do for clients."*
2. **I = personal acts / origin.** *"When I'm referring to I, I'm referring to things that I've personally done."*
3. **Will soften "you" to "business owners" / archetypes** — comfortable with indirect register.
4. **Never names a specific client.** Always calls out **vertical + location + scale** — e.g., *"this solar business in WA," "this plumbing business."*

*"I have an immediate aversion to anything that goes about guaranteeing anything because I know from someone that's been there and done it, you can't do that."*

- **First paragraph isn't the red-flag zone** — that's hook territory. Trust failure happens deeper.

### Q93: Sales-call red flags

**Crucial distinction:** ignorance ≠ red flag. Not knowing things is what the session is for.

**Real red flags:**
1. No decision-makers on the call
2. Insufficient turnover
3. No clarity on own numbers (*"surprisingly common"*)
4. No team to deliver
5. Bashing past agencies
6. Hail Mary energy (hard disqualifier)
7. Price negotiation (untested but won't budge)

### Q94: Coach / advisor vetting

All standard red flags confirmed. **Reputation is the gate before the call** — if reputation is bad, you don't get to the vetting stage.

### Q95: Product / tool vetting

Conservative on new tools — doesn't churn through products. Adds:
- **Weak trial periods** — *"try our product for 7 days when you know full well it's going to take at least a month"*

### Q96: Peer marketing claims

Limited exposure (peers don't market to competitors). Confirmed all listed flags. *"Bang on."*

### Q97: Sycophancy

1. **Client yes-saying:** often legitimate (they came for the system)
2. **Team sycophancy:** suspicious — *"I'll let them know pretty regularly"*
3. **Silent team members in meetings:** *"annoys the hell out of me"*
4. **Peer conversations:** mix — will publicly disagree with podcast guests live
5. **Actively invites perspective:** *"grown up conversation," "compare experiences"*

### Q98: Personal behaviour red flags

- **"Loose lips sink ships"** team drinks as informal character assessment
- **Rude to waitstaff = character tell** (casual condescension, not stress reaction)
- **"Precious people"** — dismissive term for fragile-ego types
- **Wealth-superiority dynamic** — flagged in others, monitored in self
- **"I'm very conscious of"** — self-monitoring phrase

### Q99: Re-evaluation triggers

- **Leaders who take all the credit instead of sharing it** — sign of poor leadership
- **Borrowed maxim from leadership consultant friend:** *"You always pass the credit onto the team and you always take responsibility for what goes wrong."*

### Q100: The Final Question — the rule above all others

**"Be honest and have integrity."**

The spine. Every other voice rule sits underneath this one.
